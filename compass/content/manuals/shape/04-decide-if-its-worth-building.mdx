You have an idea. You've defined the problem, found some competition, and identified a customer. Now the actual question: should you build this, with the time you actually have, right now?

Most founders treat this as a question about the idea — is it good, is it original, has it been done before. That's the wrong frame. Those are things you can't fully know yet. What you can know is whether five conditions for a viable early-stage product are present right now. Skip any of them and you'll find out the hard way in month four.

## The five questions

**1. Is the problem real and urgent?**
Real problems leave evidence — forum complaints, Reddit threads, spreadsheet workarounds people hate but can't stop using, money already being spent on something that half-solves it. Urgency is the other half. A real problem people want solved *someday* will produce encouraging conversations that never turn into customers. Urgent means they want it fixed this month, before the pain hits again. Quick test: ask five target users how they handle the problem today. No current workaround? Probably not urgent enough.

**2. Is the market big enough for your actual goals?**
This only matters relative to what you want. A niche tool serving 500 law firms at $200/month is a $1.2M ARR business at 10% penetration. That's a great outcome for an indie founder and a terrible outcome for a VC-backed startup. Know which game you're playing before you start — building a lifestyle business with venture-scale complexity is a fast route to miserable.

**3. Can you reach your customer?**
The best product in a market you can't access is worth less than a decent product in a market you're already part of. Do you know where these people hang out? Can you get in front of them without paying for every click from day one? If you're a designer building for designers, you start with a real advantage. If you're building for independent pharmacists and you've never met one — not a dealbreaker, but it's a real cost. Account for it honestly.

**4. Are you willing to do the boring parts?**
Once you have real users, building the product is maybe 30% of the job. The rest is support tickets at 9pm, sales calls where you explain the same thing for the twentieth time, chasing failed payments, debugging edge cases that affect two users. If you'd resent that work at scale, worth knowing now — not in month six.

**5. Will they pay instead of building it themselves?**
In 2025, this is the question most founders skip. A motivated person can vibe-code a functional version of your idea in a weekend. The products that survive this are the ones that do something the user genuinely can't replicate easily — because of integrations, data network effects, ongoing maintenance, compliance requirements, or accumulated polish from thousands of users. "I could build this" is not the same as "I will build this." But know what you're competing against.

| Question | Green light | Worth pausing |
|---|---|---|
| Is the problem real and urgent? | People have active workarounds | People agree it's annoying but aren't doing anything |
| Is the market big enough? | Fits your actual goals | You haven't defined your goals yet |
| Can you reach the customer? | You're already in their world | Starting from zero with no relationships |
| Will you do the boring parts? | Yes | You're only excited about the product |
| Will they pay instead of build? | Complex, recurring, or compliance-driven | Simple enough to replicate in a weekend with AI |

Yes to all five? Build it. Hedging on two or more? Keep digging. These questions don't get easier once you're six months in and emotionally attached.

## If you're building a mobile app

Mobile has three additional questions worth answering before you commit:

**App Store rules — does your product fit?** Both Apple and Google have content and functionality guidelines that can get an app rejected or removed. If your product involves payments between users, financial services, healthcare data, or anything adjacent to those categories, read the App Store review guidelines before you build. Finding out at submission that your core feature isn't allowed is an expensive lesson.

**The 30% cut — does your business model still work?** Apple and Google take 30% of in-app purchases and subscriptions processed through their payment systems. If you're charging $10/month and 30% goes to Apple, your economics look very different than a web subscription. Many founders route around this with web-based subscription flows, but Apple's rules on this have tightened. Know the rules before you price.

**Distribution is harder on mobile.** App Store and Play Store discovery is highly competitive and heavily weighted toward established apps with reviews and ratings. Your first mobile users will almost certainly come from your own outreach and community, not organic search. Account for this in your acquisition plan.

## One more thing: timing

An idea can be right and still be wrong for right now. Remote work tools were a good idea in 2018. They were a great idea in March 2020. Same idea, completely different outcome. Ask whether the market conditions exist today to support what you're building — not just whether it makes sense in theory.

## Checklist

- [ ] I've answered all five questions honestly
- [ ] I know whether I'm building a lifestyle business or something with venture-scale ambitions — and I've sized the market accordingly
- [ ] I've identified whether the problem is urgent enough that people act on it today
- [ ] I've assessed whether I can reach the customer without paid acquisition
- [ ] I've honestly considered whether motivated users would just build this themselves

> *Help me honestly evaluate whether I should build this product.*

> *Idea: [DESCRIBE IT]*

> *My goal: [LIFESTYLE BUSINESS / SCALE / STILL FIGURING OUT]*
>

>
> *Be direct. Tell me where the idea is strong, where it's weak, and what I should validate before committing.*
