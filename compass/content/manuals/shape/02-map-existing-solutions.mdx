If your target user has a real problem, someone is already charging them for a half-solution. Your job is to find out what that solution gets wrong.

Most founders skip this step because finding strong competitors feels threatening. It shouldn't. A competitor means the market is real and people are already paying. No competitors usually means no market. What you're looking for isn't whether competition exists — it's where it falls short.

## Where to look

- **G2 and Capterra** — the most honest reviews on the internet. Sort by 2 and 3 stars. The 1-star reviews are usually someone's support ticket that went badly. The 2 and 3-star reviews are where people describe exactly what they wish was different. That's your product brief — someone already wrote it for you.
- **App Store and Shopify App Store** — same principle, different market
- **Reddit** — find the subreddits your target user hangs out in and search for threads about the problem. People complain about tools on Reddit the way they never would in a survey or a customer interview. Raw, specific, and free.

## What you're actually looking for

Not "what does this competitor do." That's table stakes. You want the gap:

- The customer segment they've moved upmarket and stopped caring about
- The use case they've deprioritized because it didn't fit their roadmap
- The workflow they make three clicks harder than it needs to be
- The thing reviewers keep mentioning that never gets fixed

That gap is where you live. Not "we do everything they do but better" — that's a feature war you'll probably lose. "We're the version built specifically for [underserved segment]" is a position you can actually win from.

> The 2 and 3-star reviews are your product brief. Someone already wrote it. Go read it.

## Checklist

- [ ] I've identified 2-3 tools my target user is currently using
- [ ] I've read the 2 and 3-star reviews for each
- [ ] I can name the gap — what they consistently complain is missing
- [ ] I know the language real users use to describe the problem

## Prompt

> *Here's my startup idea: [DESCRIBE IT]. My target user is [DESCRIBE THEM].*
>
> *What are all the existing tools or solutions this person might currently be using to solve this problem? For each one: what it does well, what the common complaints are, and what gap it leaves that a new product could fill.*
>
> *Also tell me: is there any evidence this problem is already validated by the fact that people are paying for existing solutions?*
