You don't need a product to validate. You need a way to surface intent.

This step exists because the speed of AI tools has removed the forcing function that used to make founders think twice. Before vibe coding, you had months of development work ahead of you — which naturally made you talk to people first. Now you can build the wrong thing in a weekend. Validation doesn't take that long either.

## The methods, in order of signal strength

**Talk to people directly.** The highest-signal method. Find five people who match your target user and message them. Ask about past behavior, not hypothetical future behavior.

Good question: "Have you ever lost a client because of a missed deadline? How did you handle it?"
Bad question: "Would you use a tool that prevented that?"

People are generous with hypothetical yeses. They're honest about what they've actually done. Expect to send 20 messages to get 5 conversations. That's the funnel.

**Build a one-page site.** Name the pain, promise the outcome, add a waitlist. Share it in three communities where your target user is active. 5–10% conversion on cold traffic is real signal. Under 2% means the message isn't landing.

**Do it manually first.** The concierge method: do the thing by hand before you automate it. If you want to build automated client reporting, offer to create reports manually for five clients this week. If people engage seriously, the pain is real. If they say yes and then ghost you — also useful information.

**Pre-sell.** Post in a relevant community describing the problem and your proposed solution. Ask if anyone would pay $X for it. Link to a simple Stripe payment page or a signup form. Real intent shows up as clicks and follow-through. Replies that say "great idea" are not intent.

## Read signals like an adult

Not all validation is equal. Here's the hierarchy:

1. Someone pays money
2. Someone gives an email address unprompted
3. Someone describes the problem before you name it
4. Someone shares your page
5. Someone says "interesting" — this is basically nothing

Disconfirming signals count too. If five people tell you they've already solved this fine, that's data. Don't ignore it because it's inconvenient.

## If you're validating a mobile app idea

A few additional methods that work specifically for mobile:

**App Store competitor research.** Search the App Store and Google Play for apps solving the same problem. Read every review under four stars — same principle as G2, same product brief. Pay close attention to reviews that say "this would be perfect if only..." — that's your opening.

**Check search volume.** Tools like Sensor Tower or AppFollow show how often people search for specific terms in the App Store. If nobody's searching for your category, you have a distribution problem before you build anything. If thousands of people are searching and the top results have mediocre reviews, that's a real signal.

**Download numbers tell you if the market exists.** You can estimate competitor download numbers from App Store rankings and review counts. A competitor with 10,000 reviews and a 3.2 rating is a market full of dissatisfied customers — exactly where you want to be building.

## If signal is weak, niche down before you give up

Weak signal usually means the audience is too broad, not that the idea is dead. "Freelancers lose track of deliverables" hits a content writer, a graphic designer, and a software consultant in completely different ways. You'll get vague interest from everyone and strong interest from nobody.

Niche down until you're solving a very specific version of the problem for a very specific type of person. When you describe it to them, they'll either immediately recognize it or they won't. That's the feedback you need.

## The friend feedback trap

Your friends want you to succeed. That's exactly why their feedback is useless at this stage. Post somewhere cold. Talk to strangers who are actively dealing with the problem today. A stranger who says "I've been looking for something like this" and gives you their email without being asked is worth more than 50 supportive messages from people who know you.

## Checklist

- [ ] I've had at least 5 real conversations with people who have this problem today (not friends)
- [ ] I asked about past behavior, not hypothetical future behavior
- [ ] I've either built a landing page or posted in a relevant community to test demand publicly
- [ ] At least one person has shown strong intent: money, unprompted email, or follow-up
- [ ] If signal was weak, I've considered niching down before giving up

## Prompt

> *I'm trying to validate demand for this idea without building it: [DESCRIBE IDEA]*
> *My target user is: [DESCRIBE THEM]*
>
> *Suggest three ways I can test demand this week without writing any code. For each method, tell me: what to do, what strong signal looks like, and what weak signal looks like.*
>
> *Also write me an outreach message I could send to five potential users today — conversational, no pitch, just asking about their current experience with the problem.*
