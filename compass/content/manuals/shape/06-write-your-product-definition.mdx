If you can't explain what your product does in one sentence, you don't have a product definition problem. You have a product problem.

This is the work most founders skip because it feels like it should already be obvious. It isn't. You've been thinking about this thing for weeks. You fill in gaps automatically. You describe the mechanism when the person you're talking to needs to hear the outcome. The result: a product description that makes sense to you and nobody else.

The fix is ruthless simplification — not dumbing it down, stripping away everything that isn't the core. Everything downstream depends on getting this right: your landing page, your onboarding, your feature decisions, your pitch. If the definition is vague, all of it is vague.

## Write the one-liner

The format:

> **[Product name] helps [specific who] do [specific what] so they can [specific outcome].**

Examples:
- "Mantle helps app builders understand their billing and growth metrics so they can make faster product decisions."
- "Loom helps teams share quick video updates so they can skip the meetings that don't need to happen."

Notice what each one does: names a specific person, a specific action, and a specific outcome. Not "businesses," not "manage," not "be more productive."

**Signs your one-liner needs work:**
- It uses the words "platform," "solution," or "ecosystem"
- It takes more than one sentence
- It could describe three different products
- A stranger reads it and asks "but what does it actually do?"

## Name the core action

Every product that works has one thing it does better than anything else. Not five — one.

The core action is the single thing a user does in your product that delivers the most value. If a user could only do one thing in your product, what would make them keep paying?

- Notion: create and organize documents in one place
- Stripe: accept payments
- Loom: record and share a video

If you have five answers to this question, you have a focus problem. Pick one.

## Write three "we're not" statements

Scope is defined as much by what you exclude as what you include.

"We're a billing analytics tool for SaaS founders — not a full CRM, not a general analytics platform, not a BI tool."

This sentence tells your target user they're in the right place and tells the wrong user they're not. If you struggle to write three "we're not" statements, your scope is still too vague.

## Test the clarity

You can't test your own clarity — you're too close. Find someone who doesn't know your product:

1. Show them your one-liner (no context, no explanation)
2. Ask: "What do you think this does?"
3. Ask: "Who do you think it's for?"
4. Don't help them. Just listen.

If their answers roughly match your intent — you're clear. If they ask follow-up questions — you're not. Do this with three people who are at least two degrees removed from your world.

## Checklist

- [ ] I have a one-liner that names who, what, and the outcome
- [ ] It doesn't use "platform," "solution," or "ecosystem"
- [ ] I can name the single core action my product delivers
- [ ] I've written three "we're not" statements
- [ ] I've tested the one-liner with at least three people who don't know the product
- [ ] Their description of what it does matches mine

## Prompt

> *Here's my product one-liner: [YOUR ONE-LINER]*
> *My target user is: [DESCRIBE THEM]*
> *The core action in my product is: [WHAT THEY DO]*
>
> *Be honest:*
> *1. Is it clear who this is for?*
> *2. Is the outcome specific or generic?*
> *3. Could this describe a competitor's product without changing a word?*
> *4. What would make this sharper?*
