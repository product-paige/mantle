If you're building for everyone, you're building for no one.

"Small businesses." "Busy professionals." "Developers." These feel like real audiences. They're not — they're categories. You can't message a category. You can't find a category in a Slack community. You definitely can't build something a category loves enough to pay for every month.

Every founder thinks they understand this and then writes "our target user is anyone who needs to manage projects more efficiently." That sentence has never helped anyone build anything.

The founders who get early traction fast do the opposite: they find one specific person with one specific painful problem and make something that person would describe as the only tool that actually gets it.

## Score your segments

List every type of person who might have this problem. Don't filter yet — freelancers, agency owners, startup founders, HR teams, operators, whatever. Then rate each group across three things:

| Criterion | What it means | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Pain intensity** | How badly do they feel this? Daily frustration or occasional inconvenience? | Higher pain = more motivated to pay |
| **Willingness to pay** | Do they already pay for related tools? | Paying for workarounds = validated demand |
| **Reachability** | Can you find and talk to them without paying? | If you can't reach them, you can't validate or acquire them |

Pick the segment that scores highest across all three. That's your starting point — not your only customer forever, just your best shot at an early win.

## Get embarrassingly specific

"Freelancers" → "Freelance UX designers managing more than four concurrent client projects using a spreadsheet to track deliverables"

Read those two back to back. One of them you could find in a LinkedIn search. One of them you could write an outreach message to. One of them will read your landing page and think "this is literally for me." Specificity isn't limiting — it's how you get anyone to care.

## Find three real people who match

Not personas. Not archetypes. Actual humans with LinkedIn profiles you could click right now.

If you can't find three real people who match your description, one of two things is true: the segment is too narrow to be viable, or your description is still too vague to be useful. Either way — useful information before you spend a month building for them.

Once you have names, message them. This is also the start of 1.5.

## Checklist

- [ ] I've listed at least 8 types of people who might have this problem
- [ ] I've scored each on pain, willingness to pay, and reachability
- [ ] I've picked one segment to focus on first
- [ ] My target description is specific enough to describe a real person
- [ ] I can name three real people who match it
- [ ] I know which community or channel they're most active in

## Prompt

> *I'm trying to identify the best initial customer segment for my product.*
>
> *The problem I'm solving: [DESCRIBE IT]*
> *Potential audiences I've identified: [LIST THEM]*
>
> *For each audience, help me score them on: pain intensity, willingness to pay (do they already pay for related tools?), and reachability (are they findable in specific communities?)*
>
> *Then recommend which segment to start with and explain why.*
