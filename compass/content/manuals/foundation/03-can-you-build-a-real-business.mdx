## The skepticism is real but wrong

The argument goes: the code is messy, you don't understand what you shipped, it won't scale, real developers would never build it this way.

None of that disqualifies a business.

Messy code has shipped inside Fortune 500 companies for decades. Most early-stage products are one bad deploy away from an outage regardless of how they were built. Technically beautiful products find zero customers every day.

<Callout>
The question was never how clean the code is. The question is whether someone will pay for what you built.
</Callout>

## The revenue is already there

Pieter Levels built Nomad List and Remote OK with PHP so rough it became a meme. He crossed $1M in revenue. Nobody cared about the code.

Marc Lou went from idea to $5K MRR in under two weeks with a SaaS built almost entirely with AI tools. He ships fast and charges immediately, that's the whole playbook.

Founders using Cursor, Lovable, Bolt, and Base44 are hitting $5K–$20K MRR within 90 days of launch. Not viral flukes, small, specific tools solving real workflow problems for people who pay monthly.

The patterns that keep showing up:

- **Niche workflow tools** for a specific profession — legal, agencies, freelancers, healthcare admin. Narrow enough that the builder knows the problem personally, specific enough that generalist tools don't serve it.
- **Internal tools made external** — someone solves their own workflow problem, realizes others have it, starts charging. Already validated because the builder was the first paying customer.
- **Automation layers** on top of existing software — tools that connect things that don't connect cleanly, or add a feature a platform is missing.

One solo founder built a client-facing reporting tool for marketing agencies using Cursor and Supabase. No co-founder, no engineering background. $8K MRR in four months. The product isn't pretty under the hood. It works. People pay for it every month. That's a business.

## The real challenges nobody talks about enough

Building the app is now the easy part. Here's where people actually get stuck.

**Security gaps you don't know you have.** AI builders generate code that works. They don't always generate code that's secure. Exposed Supabase keys, missing row-level security, auth flows with holes in them, these don't show up during a demo. They show up when someone exploits them. Most vibe-coded apps ship with at least one of these. The fix is usually straightforward, you just have to know to look.

**Debugging gets painful at scale.** Fixing a bug in a 500-line app is easy. Fixing one in a 20-screen app with two months of AI-generated changes you half-remember is not. The edit-test-deploy loop gets slower as the codebase grows. Version control from day one is non-negotiable.

**Distribution is still entirely on you.** Lovable can't find you customers. Base44 can't write your cold emails. Most vibe-coded apps that fail don't fail because they were badly built, they fail because the founder had no acquisition plan.

**The duct-tape stack problem.** A solo founder running a real product is typically managing an AI builder, a database, an auth provider, a payment processor, an email service, and an analytics tool. It works until one piece changes its pricing, has an outage, or doesn't integrate cleanly with the next one. What started as a product becomes a maintenance job.

## The bottom line

The constraint was never technical sophistication, it was the cost of getting from idea to working product in front of real users. That constraint is gone.

What remains is the same thing that always separated the founders who made it from the ones who stayed in permanent beta: talking to customers relentlessly, charging real money early, and dealing with the operational layer seriously enough that the thing keeps running.
