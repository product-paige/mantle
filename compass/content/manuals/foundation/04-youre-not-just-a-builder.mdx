Building software used to be hard. Not just intellectually, logistically. You needed to know a language, a framework, how to deploy. You needed months. Sometimes years.

Now you need a weekend and a good idea.

And the tech world has decided to call the people doing this vibe coders.

It's a fun term. It's also a trap.

Calling yourself a vibe coder gives you a built-in excuse. Vibe coders don't need a pricing strategy. Vibe coders don't think about retention. Vibe coders just ship cool stuff and see what happens. It keeps things low-stakes, which feels good right up until you realize you built something people actually want and have no idea what to do next.

<Callout>
If someone is using the thing you built, you're not a vibe coder. You're a founder.
</Callout>

That word feels heavy. Ignore the baggage. Founder means you made something from nothing and you're responsible for what you built and the people who use it. You don't need investors or a business plan. You just need to start making decisions like someone who takes their own product seriously.

## What actually changes

- **You start thinking about users as your responsibility.** Not as people who stumbled across your thing, as people you're accountable to. That shift changes how you prioritize, how you respond when something breaks, and whether you build the feature that's fun or the one that actually solves their problem.
- **You start thinking about sustainability.** How does this keep existing? Can you charge for it? What would it take for this to cover its own costs? These aren't exciting questions. But they're the ones that separate the apps still around in two years from the ones that quietly disappear when the builder gets bored.
- **You start being intentional about distribution.** Building something great is necessary but not sufficient. How do more of the right people find this? Nobody is going to solve that for you.

## The procrastination patterns that kill AI-built products

The AI era has given builders an infinite supply of reasons to not launch. There's always a better model coming. Always a feature worth adding first. Always a reason the timing isn't quite right.

There isn't. Ship the thing.

<CommonFailure title="Building 10 things at once">
When spinning up a new project takes 20 minutes, the activation energy for "new idea" is basically zero. So you start everything and finish nothing. Every new project is a way to feel productive without doing the hard part. Pick one. Finish it. Launch it.
</CommonFailure>

<CommonFailure title="Scrapping before you launch">
You've been building for six weeks and suddenly all you can see is what's wrong with it. This is not quality control. It's fear wearing a productive outfit. The version you're about to scrap is good enough to learn from.
</CommonFailure>

<CommonFailure title="Waiting for one more feature">
"I just need to add X first." X is never the last thing. The feature list grows in direct proportion to how long you avoid showing the product to strangers.
</CommonFailure>

<CommonFailure title="Chasing the new tool">
A new model drops and your current project suddenly feels inadequate. Maybe you should rebuild it. Maybe the timing is off. This is procrastination with a convincing story attached.
</CommonFailure>

## How to actually finish something

<Checklist>
  <CheckItem>Set a specific ship date and tell someone, not "soon," an actual date</CheckItem>
  <CheckItem>Define "done enough to launch" before you start: one core flow, basic auth, data that persists</CheckItem>
  <CheckItem>Feature freeze, no new features until you've launched and heard from real users</CheckItem>
  <CheckItem>One day of polish, then ship it</CheckItem>
</Checklist>

<RealityCheck>
The best version of your app is the one that exists in the world with real users. Not the one still open in a Lovable tab that nobody has ever seen.
</RealityCheck>
