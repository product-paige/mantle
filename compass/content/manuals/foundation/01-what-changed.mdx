title: "What AI changed"
summary: "Building software got dramatically easier. Standing out didn't."
lastUpdated: "2026-05-13"

If you had a software idea a few years ago and weren't a developer, your options looked like this:

| Option | Reality |
| --- | --- |
| Hire developers | Expensive |
| Find a technical cofounder | Basically startup dating |
| Learn to code | Years |
| No-code tools | Hit a wall the moment you needed something custom |
| Give up on the idea | Surprisingly common |

Now there's a fifth option: build it yourself with AI as your implementation layer. Tools like Lovable, Base44, and Bolt let non-technical founders go from idea to working app in an afternoon. Tools like Cursor and GitHub Copilot let experienced developers ship in days what used to take weeks.

The bottleneck moved. It's no longer technical skill, it's product judgment. Whether you're solving a real problem, for a real person, in a way they'll actually pay for.

## The supply explosion

A lot of people figured this out at the same time. 36 million people joined GitHub last year. 121 million repositories were created in 2025. The Shopify App Store added more than 700 new apps in a single week in May 2026. Being new doesn't get you noticed anymore. Neither does being functional. Both used to be impressive on their own. Now they're table stakes.

The categories with a handful of competitors a year ago now have dozens. And the bar to ship the next one keeps getting lower.

## Good isn't enough anymore

Here's what changed for users: when you hit friction in a new product today, the first thought isn't "what else is out there." It's "could I just build this myself?" Or replace it with Claude, a Notion doc, and some duct tape.

Polished UI used to signal effort, skill, and legitimacy. Now it mostly signals someone had access to AI. A well-designed sidebar has convinced a lot of founders they had product-market fit when what they actually had was generated screens.

The products that pull ahead now compete on things that are harder to automate: clarity, positioning, trust, reliability, distribution, and taste. None of those come out of a build prompt.

## What didn't change

Here's what the tools didn't fix: distribution, monetization, retention, and operations.

You can ship an app in a weekend. You cannot ship a business in a weekend. The friction didn't disappear, it moved.

You ship the app. You get early users. And then almost immediately you realize what you actually need: auth that doesn't break, billing that handles edge cases, infrastructure that stays up, analytics that tell you what's happening, support workflows that don't live in your personal inbox. None of the tools you used to build the thing were built to help you run it.

So builders assemble stacks. An AI coding tool here, an auth provider there, a payment processor, an email service, a database, an analytics tool. It works for a while. Then one piece changes its pricing. Another has an outage. A third doesn't integrate with the fourth. What started as a product becomes a maintenance job.

## Why this matters before you build anything

The speed of building has removed the forcing function that used to slow founders down. You can now build the wrong thing in two days instead of six months. The number is smaller but the outcome is the same: you built something nobody needed.

The founders who win aren't the ones who build fastest, they're the ones who point the speed at the right target.

<Callout>
The tools give you leverage. Leverage amplifies whatever you point it at. Point it at the wrong problem and you get a very efficient path to nowhere.
</Callout>
