---
title: "Find your ideal customer"
---

Broad audiences feel safe. Bigger market, bigger upside, more optionality. On paper, it sounds smart.

Early-stage products usually experience the opposite.

When you try to serve everyone, feedback conflicts, messaging softens, and priorities get messy. You end up collecting opinions instead of traction.

Most strong products begin with a smaller group that feels the problem sharply and recognizes the value quickly. Facebook started with university students located in the US. Many products people now see as broad started narrow first.

This section will help you choose where traction should start.

---

## **Why broad audiences slow founders down**

Broad segments look good in spreadsheets. In the product, they create absolute chaos and will slow GTM down.

If your target includes too many different realities, you will hear conflicting requests, write confusing copy, and ship “nice-to-haves” for six months. The product starts getting pulled in multiple directions.

“Everyone” is an expensive customer segment.

Early momentum comes from concentrated demand: a smaller group that feels the problem often, cares now, and recognizes the win quickly.

---

## **What you’re actually choosing**

You are not picking your forever market. You are picking your **starting market**.

The goal is not to be “niche.” The goal is to be **obvious** to a group that already has the situation, the headache, and the motivation to change it.

Once you win somewhere specific, you can expand with signal instead of guesses.

---

## **Start with concentrated demand, not a big TAM**

At this stage, TAM is mostly a coping mechanism.

You want the group where:

- the problem shows up frequently
- the consequence is non-trivial (time, money, risk, stress)
- they already have a workaround (even a bad one)
- they can tell you, unprompted, what they’ve tried

Big markets are useful later. Early on, *clarity beats size*.

---

## **“Shopify merchants” is still too broad**

Many founders think they have a niche when they’ve actually only named a platform.

For example, “Shopify merchants” is not a niche. It includes:

- brand-new stores and mature brands
- one-person shops and full teams
- low-margin categories and high-margin categories
- owners who DIY everything and operators running complex workflows

A first-time founder launching their first store has very different problems than a Shopify Plus brand doing millions in revenue. A beauty brand has different needs than a supplement company managing subscriptions.

Same platform. Completely different realities.

**Same platform → Different users → Different urgency → Different budgets → Different buying process**

If your segment contains too many different worlds, feedback gets messy, messaging gets softer, and product decisions get slower.

---

## **If you cannot picture them, you do not know them**

“Freelancers” is a label.

“Freelance designers juggling five clients and tracking deliverables in spreadsheets” is a real person with a real workflow.

You should be able to picture:

- what their week looks like
- where the problem hits
- what they do right after it hits

If you cannot, your target is still too blurry to build for.

---

## **How to niche down (practical angles)**

Niching down does not mean shrinking the opportunity. It usually means **making the first win possible**. The simplest way is to narrow by *one* meaningful variable at a time.

Keep this table. Use it like a menu.

| Niching angle | What it means | Example starting market | Why it works early |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Company size | Revenue, volume, team size, complexity | Shopify brands doing $100k+/month | Similar urgency, budgets, and constraints |
| Vertical | Industry/category language + workflows | Beauty brands on Shopify | Shared context and sharper content |
| Stage | Where they are in the journey | Pre-launch founders building AI apps | Clear priorities and fast feedback |
| Geography | Region, regulation, local constraints | EU subscription brands | Built-in urgency + clear differences |
| Business model | How they make money + what they optimize | Subscription ecommerce brands | Shared KPIs, incentives, and retention needs |
| Job role | Who owns the problem day-to-day | Ecommerce retention managers | Clear buyer, clearer “job to be done” |
| Use case | One urgent outcome | Recover abandoned carts | Fast recognition and simpler onboarding |
| Pain intensity | Who is hit hardest + most often | Agencies spending hours on weekly reports | Headache drives action |

Most founders niche down once. Stronger starting markets usually come from combining **two variables**.

Examples:

- Beauty brands on Shopify doing $100k+/month
- Agencies managing 10+ stores
- Pre-launch founders building AI apps
- EU subscription brands

Shopify merchants (too broad) → Shopify beauty brands (better) → Shopify beauty brands doing $100k+/month (sharp)

<aside>

Niching down often makes marketing *cheaper* and clearer. Ads get more relevant. Content gets more specific. Outreach feels less awkward. Referrals get easier because people know exactly who to send you to.

</aside>

## **Where to actually find these people**

If your segment is real, it is findable. You should be able to name **three** real people who match it today. Not “someone like this.” Three actual humans.

Places that usually work:

- LinkedIn (job titles + company type + keywords)
- niche communities (Slack/Discord/Circle)
- Reddit (high-signal threads, not broad subreddits)
- Facebook groups (yes, still)
- industry newsletters and podcasts (who comments, who sponsors, who shows up)
- your existing network (warm intros are underrated)

If you cannot find them, either the segment is too vague—or you do not understand the map yet.

---

## **Talk to buyers, not just “users”**

Early traction shows up when you talk to the people who *own* the consequence.

Sometimes that is the user. Sometimes it is their manager. Sometimes it is the founder who is tired of the mess.

Start with simple outreach:

> Hey [name], I’m researching how [segment] handles [problem]. What do you do today, and what still feels annoying or costly?
> 

You are listening for:

- repeating situations (not one-off complaints)
- existing workarounds
- “we already tried…” stories
- urgency (“we need this soon,” “this keeps happening,” “it’s a mess”)

Compliments are cheap. Specific stories are signal.

---

## **Common mistakes here**

- **Choosing access over urgency.** Building for people you know is convenient, not always smart.
- **Going broad to feel safer.** It usually just delays learning.
- **Letting edge-case interest steer the roadmap.** A few random sign-ups are not your market.

If feedback is all over the place, it is not a product problem yet. It is usually a target problem.

---

## **Quick scorecard**

Your starting market is strong if:

- [ ]  they feel the problem often
- [ ]  they can describe it clearly (without you coaching them)
- [ ]  they already have a workaround
- [ ]  you know where to find more of them
- [ ]  they recognize value quickly

If several are missing, narrow further.

---

## **The takeaway**

Pick the group that feels this problem most, right now.

Concentrated demand beats a big story. A sharp target gives you better conversations, clearer messaging, faster iteration, and easier distribution.

Audience clarity comes before scale.
