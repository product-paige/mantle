## **Start with the problem**

In the AI era, ideas are cheap. The average person can turn almost anything into a prototype, landing page, or half-working app quickly. The advantage is no longer *having* an idea. It is choosing a problem worth solving.

Good products are built on headaches people already feel, complain about, workaround, or pay to reduce.

If nobody is inconvenienced, delayed, losing money, or mildly annoyed on a regular basis, it may not be an idea worth solving.

This chapter helps you separate interesting ideas from real demand.

---

## Why this matters

A fuzzy problem creates fuzzy everything.

Your messaging turns into “for everyone.” Your roadmap becomes vibes. Your validation calls get polite compliments and no follow-up.

A clean problem statement is not copywriting. It is decision-making.

---

## The difference between “annoying” and “expensive to ignore”

Some problems are just texture. They bother people, but not enough to change behavior. Real problems pull time, money, or attention *every week*—and people already pay a tax to live with them.

A quick gut-check:

- **Mild annoyance:** “Yeah, that’s kind of a pain.” (Then nothing changes.)
- **Recurring headache:** “We deal with that constantly.” (Then they describe the workaround.)
- **Expensive to ignore:** “If we don’t fix this, we lose ___.” (Then they get specific without prompting.)

If users shrug, that is feedback.

---

## What a strong problem sounds like

Good problem framing is concrete. It has a *who*, a *moment*, and a *cost*.

| Weak framing | Stronger framing |
| --- | --- |
| Small businesses need better marketing tools | Shopify brands with repeat customers lose revenue because no post-purchase winback emails are set up |
| Reporting is annoying | Founders check three tools every week just to understand churn |
| Onboarding is confusing | Free users sign up, then stall because setup is unclear |

Notice what the stronger versions do:

- They point to a repeated situation.
- They name the consequence.
- They make the “so what?” obvious.

You are not trying to sound smart. You are trying to be *testable*.

---

## Stop solving founder boredom

A lot of “startup ideas” are just a reaction to boredom, curiosity, or a new tool. That is fine for a weekend project. It is a terrible filter for what to build next.

A useful question:

**Would anyone be relieved if this existed?** Not impressed. Relieved.

“Cool idea” has buried many weekends.

---

## Find the recurring moment (the loop)

Strong problems usually show up in a loop:

- a weekly report that always turns into a scramble
- a handoff that always drops details
- a workflow that always requires a spreadsheet “just for now”
- a task that always needs a human to babysit it

You are looking for the moment that keeps repeating, not the one-off frustration.

**Exercise:** Write the loop in one sentence: *“Every time  **happens,**  breaks, and it costs ___.”*

---

## Workarounds are demand wearing a trench coat

If a problem is real, people rarely do nothing. They *patch it*.

Workarounds are a gift. They show what people will tolerate—and what they will consistently spend effort to avoid.

Common “demand signals”:

- spreadsheets that keep growing tabs
- SOPs that exist because “the tool doesn’t handle it”
- copying data between systems
- weekly calendar blocks just to keep things from falling apart
- “We hired a person to do this” (the loudest signal)

When someone says, “We use three tools and a spreadsheet to make this work,” pay attention.

---

## A simple discernment filter: frequency × consequence

You do not need a 10-category scoring model here. You need a clean filter.

Ask two questions:

1) **How often does this happen?** (daily, weekly, monthly)  

2) **What happens if nothing changes?** (time loss, revenue loss, risk, stress, churn)

- High frequency + low consequence is busywork.
- Low frequency + high consequence can be a wedge (if it is repeated across a market).
- High frequency + high consequence is where serious products live.

**Exercise:** Write both answers in plain language. No numbers needed. Just honest.

---

## What to do when the “problem” is actually a feature request

If someone describes the solution before the situation, you probably have a feature request, not a problem.

Bring it back to reality:

- “When does that come up?”
- “What happens right before it?”
- “What breaks if you don’t do it?”
- “What do you do today instead?”

If they cannot answer, it is not a strong problem yet. It is a preference.

---

## Quick checklist

- [ ]  I can describe the recurring moment (the loop)
- [ ]  I can name the consequence (time, money, risk, stress)
- [ ]  I can point to a current workaround (even a messy one)
- [ ]  I can explain why “doing nothing” is costly
- [ ]  I can tell the difference between “neat” and “necessary”

---

## The takeaway

Start with what is broken in the real world.

If the problem is real, people already feel it, complain about it, and work around it. Your job is not to invent demand. It is to recognize it—before you build the world’s most polished solution to something nobody cares about.
