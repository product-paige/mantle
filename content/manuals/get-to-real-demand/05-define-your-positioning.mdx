## Most founders don’t have a traffic problem. They have a clarity problem.

If the right buyer lands on your page and still can’t explain what you do, more traffic only wastes money.

Positioning is not what you say. It is what buyers understand.

Buyers do not “read” your homepage. They scan for a reason to care:

- *What is this?*
- *Is it for someone like me?*
- *Will it make my life meaningfully easier (or my numbers meaningfully better)?*
- *What would I stop doing if I bought it?*
- *Why deal with this now?*

If your message doesn’t answer those fast, the buyer moves on. Not because they are lazy. Because they are busy and already have a workaround.

---

## How buyers decide (in under 10 seconds)

Most evaluation follows a simple sequence:

1. **Categorize** — “What kind of thing is this?”
2. **Relate** — “Is this for someone in my situation?”
3. **Payoff** — “What improves if it works?”
4. **Switch cost** — “What replaces what I do today?”
5. **Urgency** — “Why should I care now?”

Buyers categorize first. Evaluate second.

Your job is to make those five steps feel obvious.

---

## The positioning triangle: Category + persona + outcome

Good early positioning is built from three pieces:

- **Category** — the mental shortcut (CRM, booking tool, churn analytics, onboarding checklist)
- **Persona** — a specific situation, not a vague audience
- **Outcome** — what changes after purchase (functional *and* emotional)

If one of these is missing, buyers fill in the blanks with the safest assumption: “Probably not for me.”

<aside>

The goal is not to impress someone. It’s to help the right buyer self-qualify quickly.

</aside>

---

## Persona beats audience (situations win)

“Founders” is not a persona. “Teams” is not a persona. “Ecommerce brands” is a category label, not a buying situation.

Situational personas do two things:

1) They make your message sharper.

2) They make your product feel more real.

Examples:

| Too broad | Situational persona |
| --- | --- |
| Founders | Solo Shopify founder doing support + ops alone, trying to stop churn from support delays |
| Marketing teams | PMM at a Series A SaaS team with no design support, rebuilding onboarding messaging before a new pricing launch |
| Clinics | Clinic owner losing bookings to no-shows, still doing reminders manually |
| Agencies | Agency lead sending weekly reports manually, terrified of looking disorganized in front of enterprise clients |

The more situational the buyer, the stronger the message.

---

## Outcomes: functional + emotional

Founders default to functional outcomes:

- save time
- increase revenue
- reduce churn
- ship faster

Buyers also want emotional outcomes:

- feel in control
- stop looking disorganized
- avoid mistakes
- feel ahead (not behind)
- reduce stress
- look smarter internally

If you only describe the functional outcome, you sound like everyone else. The emotional outcome is often where the “I want this” comes from.

---

## What would this replace?

Buyers don’t compare your product to your competitors first.

They compare it to what they already do.

Your positioning should make the switch feel obvious: **what changes after purchase**.

Common “replacements”:

| What they do today | What they’re trying to avoid | How you should frame the switch |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Spreadsheet + weekly manual updates | Errors, stale data, “we’ll fix it later” chaos | Replace fragile tracking with an always-up-to-date system |
| Stitching together 3–5 tools | Broken workflows, tool sprawl, blame | Replace duct tape with one clean workflow |
| Internal workflow + Slack reminders | Things falling through the cracks | Replace memory-based ops with a reliable process |
| Hiring an assistant (or becoming the assistant) | Busywork, context switching | Replace human glue work with automation |
| Doing nothing (status quo) | Compounding mess, slow leaks | Replace “we’ll deal with it later” with a controlled fix |

This section is where buyers feel the *before/after*.

If you can’t name what you replace, buyers assume you’re “nice to have.”

---

## Why now? (urgency without hype)

Even clear products stall if the pain feels optional.

“Someday” kills deals.

Practical urgency comes from timing and compounding cost:

- before Q4 planning locks priorities
- before churn compounds another month
- before headcount freezes make manual work permanent
- before wasted ad spend keeps leaking
- before the process scales badly (and breaks loudly)
- before a launch, migration, or pricing change makes the mess visible

A good “why now” doesn’t yell. It points to the bill that’s already accruing.

---

## Why specificity wins

Generic positioning sounds safer because it keeps options open.

Usually, it lowers response.

Specificity usually outperforms elegance.

Compare:

| Generic | Sharper |
| --- | --- |
| Booking software for businesses | Book more massage clients without back-and-forth |
| Analytics for teams | Revenue analytics for Shopify apps |
| AI tool for founders | AI assistant for non-technical founders launching SaaS |

Specificity helps a buyer think: *This might be for me.*

That reaction matters more than broad appeal early on.

## Homepage smell test

If your headline could belong to 20 competitors, it’s weak.

Weak headlines (you’ve seen these):

- Modern growth platform
- AI productivity suite
- Better workflows for teams

The problem is not that they’re “wrong.”

The problem is they don’t help a buyer categorize, relate, or imagine the switch.

Better headlines force clarity by including **a situation + a payoff + a constraint**:

- “Reduce Shopify app churn without stitching together five tools”
- “Automate clinic reminders so no-shows stop eating your schedule”
- “Onboarding messaging for Series A SaaS teams without design support”

If your homepage needs narration, positioning is weak.

---

## The 5-second test

Show your homepage, one-liner, or product concept to someone in your target market.

Ask them to answer these within five seconds:

- What is this?
- Who is it for?
- What problem does it solve?
- What would it replace?
- Why should I care now?

If they hesitate, guess, or scroll for the answer, clarity needs work.

That is useful feedback. Use it.

---

## Write your first one-liner (two formulas)

Start with a simple working statement.

**Formula A (classic):**

> [Product] helps [persona] solve [pain] so they can [outcome].
> 

**Formula B (higher-converting early):**

> [Product] helps [persona] solve [pain] **without** [current workaround].
> 

Example:

> We help Shopify app founders reduce churn without stitching together five tools.
> 

Why “without” is powerful:

- it names the buyer’s current behavior (so it feels real)
- it implies switching cost relief (“finally, I can stop doing that”)
- it makes the alternative look worse without trash-talking competitors

Then tighten it.

Remove filler words. Remove jargon. Remove anything you wouldn’t say out loud.

Clear usually beats clever.

## Common mistakes

### Trying to sound bigger than you are

Broad often sounds weaker, not stronger.

### Leading with features

Features explain *how*. Buyers first care *why*.

### Inventing a new category too early

Familiar language usually converts better than clever labels.

### Changing copy every three days

Give messaging enough time to gather real signal.

### Talking like a company instead of a person

Corporate wording is easy to ignore.

---

## Quick positioning exercise

Fill these in fast. Do not overthink it.

- We help **[persona]**
- Solve **[pain in their words]**
- Using **[category / approach]**
- So they can **[functional outcome]**
- And they feel **[emotional outcome]**
- Unlike **[current workaround]**, we **[difference]**
- We’re worth doing now because **[why now]**

Messy first drafts are fine.

Silence on the page is worse.

---

## Quick checklist

- [ ]  A buyer can categorize us in one sentence
- [ ]  The persona is situational (not a demographic label)
- [ ]  The payoff is clear (functional + emotional)
- [ ]  We say what we replace
- [ ]  We give a credible “why now”
- [ ]  The wording sounds human
- [ ]  Someone could repeat it to another person

---

## FAQs

### What if I am still changing the product?

Normal. Early positioning should evolve. It only needs to be clear enough to test.

### What if there are multiple use cases?

Choose one main entry point first. Focus beats flexibility early.

### What if I do not know the persona yet?

Talk to more real people. Good positioning usually comes from conversations, not brainstorming.

### What if the product does many things?

Lead with the most valuable first win.

### Do I need branding first?

No. Clear positioning matters more than polished visuals early on.

---

## The takeaway

Positioning is not what you say. It is what buyers understand.

Make it easy for the right buyer to:

- categorize you
- see themselves in it
- imagine the switch
- feel urgency
- move to the next step

More traffic cannot fix a confusing message.
