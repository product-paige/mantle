# **The new alternative**

For a long time, software mostly competed against the same things: existing tools, spreadsheets, manual work, or doing nothing. That is no longer the full picture.

Today, many users have another option before they ever buy your product. They can try to solve the problem themselves.

That might mean using AI tools, combining no-code software, hiring a freelancer, asking an internal team member, or patching together a workflow that feels good enough.

This changes how demand works. You are not only competing with competitors anymore. You are competing with DIY.

---

## **Why this matters now**

Building software became faster and cheaper. That is great news for app founders, but it also means users have more ways to avoid buying software altogether.

Problems that once clearly needed a dedicated product can now be partially solved with:

- ChatGPT or Claude + spreadsheets
- Notion + automations
- Airtable + forms
- Zapier + existing tools
- a freelancer-built internal tool

If the workaround feels good enough, many people stop there.

> Building got easier for founders. It also got easier for users.
> 

---

## **What this means for demand**

A problem can be real and still not be a strong business opportunity. That usually happens when the pain exists, but the easiest available workaround is already acceptable.

Strong demand often appears when the problem is:

| Signal | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Frequent | Keeps returning until solved |
| Expensive | Costs time, money, or missed revenue |
| Risky | Mistakes matter |
| Multi-step | Harder to patch together cleanly |
| Team-based | More people involved creates friction |
| Ongoing | Needs a repeatable system |

Minor inconveniences are easier to ignore or solve casually.

---

## **Where DIY wins**

Some categories are naturally easier for users to solve themselves.

Examples:

- simple content generation
- lightweight internal trackers
- one-step automations
- basic calculators
- generic summarizers
- simple templates

These are not impossible markets but they face stronger self-serve alternatives. If a user can recreate most of the value in an afternoon, willingness to pay for an app will drop.

---

## **Where products still win**

Dedicated products usually win when they remove pain that patchwork solutions create.

That often includes:

- reliability
- speed at scale
- better UX
- deeper automation
- collaboration
- integrations
- trust and security
- reduced maintenance

People tolerate hacks until the hacks become annoying or break (which happens often). That frustration is often the real buying moment.

---

## **Use the DIY test**

Before building, ask these questions honestly:

| Question | What a strong answer looks like |
| --- | --- |
| Could users solve this themselves in a day? | No, or only partially |
| Would the DIY version break over time? | Yes |
| Are mistakes expensive here? | Yes |
| Does this involve multiple people or systems? | Yes |
| Is ongoing maintenance annoying? | Yes |
| Would someone gladly pay to avoid this? | Yes |

You are not trying to eliminate DIY options. You are trying to understand what you must beat.

---

## **How to beat the AI alternative**

You do not need to be impossible to copy. You need to be clearly better than patchwork.

That usually means winning on one or more of these:

- faster time to value
- cleaner experience
- less manual work
- stronger outcomes
- fewer mistakes
- easier collaboration
- more trust
- less maintenance

Convenience alone is often weak. Clear improvement is stronger.

---

## **Common founder mistakes**

**Comparing only against competitors** → Your real competitor may be a spreadsheet and a prompt.

**Overvaluing novelty** → Being AI-powered does not automatically create demand.

**Solving pain that is too minor** → Small annoyances rarely create urgency.

**Ignoring maintenance pain** → A workaround may seem fine at first and become painful later. That can be your opportunity.

**Assuming users want another tool** → Many users want fewer tools, not more.

---

## **Quick examples**

| Weak opportunity | Stronger opportunity |
| --- | --- |
| AI email rewriter | AI sales assistant connected to CRM, personalization, and workflow automation |
| Basic report generator | Automated reporting tied to multiple data sources with alerts and approvals |
| Habit tracker | Private transformation tracker with accountability, progress visibility, and plans |
| Simple booking calendar | Vertical booking platform built for salons with reminders, upsells, and staff workflows |

Same general category. Stronger reason to choose it.

---

## **Quick checklist**

- [ ]  I considered DIY as a competitor
- [ ]  My product solves more than mild inconvenience
- [ ]  I know where patchwork solutions fail
- [ ]  My product creates clear ongoing value
- [ ]  I can explain why someone would pay instead of build
- [ ]  I am solving pain strong enough to beat “good enough”

---

## **The takeaway**

The competitor is no longer just competitors. It is prompts, spreadsheets, freelancers, internal tools, and doing nothing. If users can solve it themselves easily, demand may be weaker than it looks. 

Build where DIY starts to fail.
