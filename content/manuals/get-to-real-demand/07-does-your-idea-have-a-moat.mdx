---
title: "Does your idea have a moat?"
---

Getting demand matters. Keeping demand matters more.

Many ideas can attract attention for a moment. Far fewer can defend that attention once competitors notice, copycats appear, or buyers decide to solve the problem another way.

That is where moats matter. And yes, you may be thinking: *I barely have a product. Why are we talking about this now?*

Because weak products can get copied faster than ever. If you only build surface-level advantages, they may not last. 

You do not need a perfect moat on day one. But it is smart to build toward one early.

---

## **A moat is staying power (not startup jargon)**

**What is a Moat? →** A moat is whatever makes your product **harder to replace** once it works. Traction alone is fragile. Attention is rented, while moats help you keep it.

In practice, durability usually comes from things that compound: trust, workflows, integrations, retained habits, accumulated data, and “we’re not switching again” fatigue.

Being first is overrated. Staying useful is underrated.

---

## **Three outcomes that signal real durability**

Strong moats tend to create one or more of these outcomes:

| Outcome | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Hard to copy | Others can’t recreate it quickly or cheaply |
| Hard to leave | It’s embedded in workflows, data, habits, or integrations |
| Hard to dismiss | Trust, reputation, or risk makes “good enough” unacceptable |

If users can leave easily, the moat may be imaginary.

---

## **Why features are a weak edge now**

Features can trend, trust compounds and in most categories, a prettier clone is still a threat. UI can be mimicked. Feature lists can be matched. “AI-powered” can be copied by Tuesday.

Your edge has to live deeper than the surface.

---

## **Indie-friendly moats that actually compound**

You do not need billion-dollar network effects to build staying power.

The most realistic “small product” moats look like this:

- **Niche expertise:** You understand a narrow market’s constraints better than general tools.
- **Workflow integration:** You plug into the messy middle (handoffs, approvals, reporting, compliance) where switching is painful.
- **Trust in a narrow market:** You become the safe choice for a specific kind of buyer.
- **Proprietary data over time:** You accumulate benchmarks, patterns, or outcomes that improve the product.
- **Loyal audience:** Distribution that you own lowers reliance on rented attention.
- **Speed through focus:** You ship faster because you’re not trying to serve everyone.

The question to keep asking:

> If this works, what gets stronger every year?
> 

---

## **Examples of strong moats in SaaS**

The best SaaS products usually stack more than one moat.

| Company | Core moat | Why it’s strong |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Wealthsimple | Trust + ecosystem | Users trust them with money. Accounts, investing, and tax products increase stickiness. |
| Stripe | Infrastructure + integrations | Deeply embedded in payment flows. Replacing it creates engineering and business risk. |
| Shopify | Ecosystem + switching costs | Stores run operations through themes, apps, payments, and workflows. |
| Notion | Workflow lock-in | Docs, systems, and internal processes become embedded over time. |
| Figma | Collaboration network effects | Shared files, comments, and libraries make team switching harder. |
| HubSpot | Distribution + product suite | Strong content engine plus connected tools across marketing, sales, and support. |

Most elite products do not rely on one magic moat. They combine trust, integrations, workflow lock-in, data, ecosystem effects, and distribution.

---

## **A simple moat test**

Answer these honestly:

- **What would a copycat NOT be able to replicate quickly?**
- **What would make a customer say “we’re not switching”?**
- **What risk do you remove that a cheaper clone can’t?**
- **What gets better with every customer or every month of usage?**

If you can’t answer yet, that’s fine. But don’t pretend the moat is “AI” or “more features.” Those are table stakes now.

---

## **Common mistakes**

- **Confusing traction with durability** (growth can be real and still be fragile)
- **Relying on features alone** (copyable)
- **Assuming AI is the moat** (it isn’t)
- **Ignoring retention** (if users churn easily, you’re exposed)
- **Overpricing into switching risk** (buyers tolerate less when alternatives are “good enough”)
- **Trying to look broad too early** (you lose the focus that creates leverage)
