# **Validate your app idea**

Many products do not fail because they lacked features. They fail because nobody cared enough.

Validation is not about proving your idea is ground breaking. It is about gathering enough real evidence to justify building further.

Most founders look for encouragement. Smart founders look for movement. 

This section helps you tell the difference.

---

## **What validation actually is**

Validation is behavior, not compliments.

Likes, “this is cool,” and polite curiosity are feel-good signals. They also cost nothing.

Real evidence shows up when someone *does* something: replies with detail, books time, makes an intro, signs up for free, pays, or follows up without you chasing them.

> **Rule:** If nobody changes behavior, nothing was validated.
> 

---

## **Why founders misread signals**

Founders usually do not get validation wrong because they are lazy. They get it wrong because they become attached to the idea before they test it.

That creates bias. Validation only works when you are willing to hear the idea may not be strong enough yet.

- asking leading questions (“would you use…?”)
- counting interest as intent
- treating one signup as traction
- ignoring silence because it’s inconvenient

If you already decided it *has to work*, you stop testing and start looking for reassurance.

---

## **Curiosity vs commitment**

Curiosity sounds like: “Interesting.” “Keep me posted.” “I might use this.”

Commitment sounds like: “When can I try it?” “Can you help me with this next week?” “Here’s how we do it today.” “I’ll pay if it works.”

If you’re not seeing commitment, don’t negotiate with the data. Tighten the idea, tighten the segment, or move on.

---

## **Fast validation vs real validation**

You usually need both. They answer different questions. 

Fast validation tells you: **Is this problem common, current, and costly enough to complain about publicly?**  

Real validation tells you: **Will anyone act when you offer help?**

| Type | Where it lives | What it’s good for | What it cannot prove |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Fast evidence | Forums, reviews, communities, public complaints | Language, patterns, frequency, “what people hate” | Whether anyone will commit |
| Real evidence | Calls, pilots, pre-sells, intros, repeated follow-ups | Urgency, constraints, buying behavior, adoption friction | Scale (yet) |

Use fast evidence to avoid obvious dead ends. Use real evidence to decide whether building deeper is rational. A short interview with a real person, often beats hours of research.

---

## **Test demand without overbuilding**

You do not need a product to test demand. You need a credible way for someone to take a step.

Good low-build tests:

- landing page + waitlist (but measure replies, not just signups)
- agency/concierge/manual service (you do it by hand first)
- clickable prototype (to test comprehension + workflow)
- beta offer with a clear outcome (time saved, revenue protected, risk reduced)

Weak test:

> “Would you use this?”
> 

Better test:

> “Want me to help you solve this in the next 2 weeks?”
> 

---

## **Signal ladder: what counts**

Not all actions are equal. You’re looking for steps that have a cost: time, reputation, money, or switching effort.

| Action | What it usually means | Strength |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Click | Momentary attention | Low |
| Email signup | Light interest | Medium |
| Detailed reply / booked call | Problem is live + they’ll invest time | Strong |
| Pilot / trial with real data | They want a result, not a demo | Very strong |
| Payment (even small) | They believe it’s worth paying to remove the headache | Highest |

The goal is not to “collect signups.” The goal is to create proof that people will move.

---

## **Strong vs weak evidence**

| Strong evidence | Weak evidence |
| --- | --- |
| They pay (or ask how to pay) | “Cool idea” |
| They introduce you to someone else | Passive likes |
| They follow up without prompting | “Maybe someday” |
| They share workflow details and constraints | Generic encouragement |

Negative feedback is also data:

- “We already solved this.”
- “This is annoying, but not worth changing.”
- “We’d just do it manually.”

That isn’t failure. That’s you avoiding a longer, more expensive failure.

---

## **When to stop validating and start building**

You do not need certainty. You need enough proof that continuing is rational. 

A good “go build” threshold usually looks like:

- the same situation shows up across multiple conversations
- people describe a current workaround (and dislike it)
- at least a few take a real step (call, pilot, pre-sell, intro, payment)
- you can keep reaching more of them without heroic effort

At some point, building becomes the next test.

## **Prompt: Validate fast with Reddit research**

Use real complaints, frustrations, and language from Reddit to pressure-test whether your idea solves a live problem.

- **Prompt: Validate fast with Reddit research**
    
    Act as an experienced product strategist.
    
    I want to validate an app idea quickly using Reddit discussions.
    
    My idea: [INSERT IDEA]
    
    Target user: [INSERT USER TYPE]
    
    Your job is to analyze whether this problem appears real, painful, and active based on Reddit-style public discussion patterns.
    
    Please give me:
    
    ### 1. Where to search on Reddit
    
    Suggest the best subreddits where this audience likely talks.
    
    ### 2. What to search
    
    Give me 10 exact Reddit search queries to uncover:
    
    - complaints
    - recurring frustrations
    - workarounds
    - tool recommendations
    - switching pain
    - buying intent
    - “I wish this existed” moments
    
    ### 3. What strong validation looks like
    
    Tell me what signals would indicate real demand.
    
    Examples:
    
    - repeated complaints
    - emotional language
    - people spending money already
    - ugly manual workarounds
    - frequent recommendation requests
    - urgency
    
    ### 4. What weak validation looks like
    
    Tell me signals that suggest weak demand.
    
    Examples:
    
    - mild annoyance only
    - rare mentions
    - no urgency
    - easy DIY solutions
    - no money being spent
    
    ### 5. Extract likely user language
    
    Give me phrases real users might use so I can improve landing page copy.
    
    ### 6. Honest verdict
    
    Based on this type of problem, rate likely demand from 1–10.
    
    ### 7. Best pivot if weak
    
    If the idea feels weak, suggest a sharper niche, stronger audience, or more painful angle.
    
    Be brutally honest.
    
    I do not need encouragement. I need signal.
    

---

## **Quick checklist**

- [ ]  I’m seeing actions, not just opinions
- [ ]  I can separate curiosity from commitment
- [ ]  I used fast evidence to spot patterns
- [ ]  I ran at least one test that asked for a real step
- [ ]  I know what “strong evidence” looks like for this product
- [ ]  I have enough proof that building deeper is rational

---

## **The takeaway**

Validation is not applause. It is movement.

If nobody takes a step, you did not validate anything—you entertained them.
