## **Capture early users**

Many founders think first users show up after launch — hit "PUBLISH" and the internet politely delivers customers.

In real life, first customers come from direct effort before anything feels scalable: conversations, outreach, referrals, manual help, and a small group of humans telling other humans.

People can't buy what they've never heard of, and they rarely care just because something exists.

This chapter shows you how to go get real momentum on purpose.

---

## **The job right now: create contact**

If you do not have users yet, you do not have a growth problem. You have a *contact* problem.

Your job is to get close enough to the market that people can:

- hear what you’re working on
- react to it (with interest or indifference)
- take a small next step

Waiting quietly is not a launch strategy.

---

## **Founder-led traction (the only kind that works pre-scale)**

At this stage, the founder is the distribution.

Not forever. Just for now.

You are earning three things you cannot buy with tools:

- access (you can actually reach people)
- trust (they take you seriously)
- signal (they reveal what they’ll do, not what they’ll say)

Traffic cannot rescue weak interest. It only amplifies it.

---

## **Outreach that doesn’t feel like outreach**

The goal is a helpful conversation, not an instant conversion.

Relevance beats polish. Short beats long. Specific context gets replies.

Three rules:

- Make it obvious why you chose them.
- Ask about their reality, not your product.
- Keep the ask small (10 minutes, one question, one quick reply).

Example:

> Hey [name] — saw you mention [specific situation]. I’m talking with [people like you] about how they handle this today. Open to a quick chat this week? I’m mapping what’s actually happening.
> 

Simple follow-up:

> Quick one: how are you handling [problem] right now?
> 

If the message reads like a pitch, you’ll get pitch-level responses: polite, vague, unusable.

---

## **Conversations are acquisition**

A good early conversation does two jobs at once:

1. It teaches you what people care about.
2. It earns permission to follow up.

Do not treat these as “research calls” that disappear into a notes folder. Treat them like the first step of a relationship.

Ask questions that force real detail:

- Walk me through the last time this happened.
- What did you do right after?
- What have you tried already?
- What did you settle for (even though you hate it)?
- If this vanished tomorrow, would you notice?

Listen for:

- repeated moments (patterns)
- messy workarounds (effort)
- urgency (timing)
- willingness to change (action)

Efficient ignorance is worse than inefficient learning.

---

## **Help people before the product exists (make this your unfair advantage)**

You do not need software to create value first. You need a result someone actually wants.

One of the fastest ways to earn demand is to solve the pain manually for a few people—then watch what they pull you toward.

Ways to “ship” before you ship:

- **Concierge service:** you do it with them, end-to-end.
- **Done-for-you workflow:** you run the process, they get the outcome.
- **Manual reports:** you build the report by hand and deliver it weekly.
- **Expert help:** you advise, audit, rewrite, or set it up with them live.
- **No-code behind the scenes:** scrappy tooling that looks like magic to them.
- **Paid pilots:** a simple paid engagement with a clear promised outcome.

This approach teaches you:

- what outcome matters most (not what features sound nice)
- what “done” actually means to them
- what they’ll tolerate (and what they refuse)
- what they will pay for *before* it’s automated
- what should become product later

Many products begin as services wearing software clothing.

If people won’t accept help manually, software will not fix the underlying “do they care?” problem.

---

## **Referrals: the early growth loop that actually compounds**

At the end of a useful conversation, ask:

> Is there anyone else dealing with this who I should speak with?
> 

This is not a “growth hack.” It’s basic social physics:

- people trust people
- warm intros beat another round of cold messages
- the right niche is usually smaller than you think

You don’t need a viral loop. You need three introductions from the first person who actually cared.

---

## **Go where demand already hangs out**

Do not force attention from random audiences. Find density.

Good places to look:

- niche Slack/Discord groups where the same pain shows up weekly
- subreddits with recurring “how do I…” threads
- LinkedIn comment sections where your buyers argue in public
- newsletters your market already trusts
- communities where people ask for tool recommendations

Small focused rooms beat massive generic audiences more often than founders want to admit.

---

## **What counts as real momentum**

Do not overvalue vanity metrics. Early demand is behavior, not applause.

| Weak signal | Strong signal |
| --- | --- |
| Likes | Replies with detail |
| Passive signups | Books time |
| “Cool idea” | Introduces someone else |
| Follows you | Asks when it’s available |
| Clicks | Pays for help |

The goal is not traffic. The goal is movement.

---

## **Do unscalable things on purpose**

Early momentum is manual and slightly awkward. That’s fine. That’s the point.

Useful unscalable moves:

- personal outreach with context
- onboarding calls before you have onboarding
- solving the problem one person at a time
- rewriting your message after every real conversation
- following up like a human
- creating a “micro-result” in 24–48 hours for someone

Later, systems matter more. Right now, learning and trust matter more.

---

## **When to move into scale**

Scale is what you earn *after* you’ve found repeatable interest.

Start thinking about SEO, ads, partnerships, and automation when:

- a clear audience repeatedly responds (without you begging)
- your message works consistently (you can predict who will say yes)
- people understand the value fast (no 20-minute explanations)
- referrals start happening naturally
- wins feel repeatable
- some retention exists (people come back, or ask for more)

Until then, keep it founder-led. It’s not “small.” It’s precise.

---

## **Quick checklist**

- [ ]  I have had real conversations with people in the target market
- [ ]  I can describe the problem in their words (not mine)
- [ ]  A few people took a next step (call, intro, pilot, payment)
- [ ]  I delivered value manually at least once
- [ ]  I asked for referrals after useful conversations
- [ ]  I know where this audience already gathers
- [ ]  I can point to behavior-based proof (not vanity metrics)

---

## **The takeaway**

Your first users usually come from effort, not systems.

Talk to real people. Help a few manually. Learn what matters. Repeat.

Once real interest becomes repeatable, growth becomes a lever.
