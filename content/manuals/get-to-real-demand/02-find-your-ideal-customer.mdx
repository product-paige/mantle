# Find your ideal customer

Most founders think they need more people interested. Usually, they just need fewer people who care more.

Early traction rarely comes from broad appeal. It comes from a small group who feels the problem sharply, recognizes themselves in the messaging, and gets value quickly.

That is why "everyone" is such an expensive audience.

When you try to attract everyone, you end up building for no one. Messaging gets softer. Features get broad. Product decisions get messier. Feedback gets harder to trust.

Many of the biggest products started narrower than people remember. Facebook was originally for university students. Then they proved strong demand in a focused group, and expanded outward.

This section is about finding the people most likely to care now, not someday.

---

## Why broad audiences fail

Broad audiences feel safer because they keep your options open. They also make the TAM look bigger, and bigger markets mean bigger revenue.

In reality, broad audiences often slow everything down.

When you target everyone, different users want different things. Feedback conflicts, priorities get messy and the product starts getting pulled in multiple directions.

Broad audiences also weaken messaging. People need to feel a product was made for them. Generic language rarely creates that reaction.

Early-stage products usually win with concentrated demand, not wide awareness. A smaller group who really cares is more valuable than a large group who only kind of does.

---

## Start with concentrated pain

You are not looking for the biggest market first. You are looking for the group that feels the pain most intensely.

The best early customers often share three traits:

- the problem happens often
- they already want it solved
- they will notice value quickly

That combination matters more than total market size at the start.

A small group with real urgency is usually better than a huge group with mild interest.

---

## "Shopify merchants" is still too broad

Many founders think they have chosen a niche, when they've only chosen a platform.

An example would be "Shopify merchants" which sounds niche and specific. It's actually not.

A new merchant launching their first store has very different problems than a Plus brand doing millions in revenue. A beauty founder has different needs than a supplement brand managing subscriptions.

**Same platform → Different users → Different urgency → Different software budgets → Different buying experience**

If your audience contains too many different realities, it is still too broad.

---

## How to niche down (practical angles)

Many founders hear "niche down" and assume it means limiting growth. Usually, it means creating growth.

Niching down does not mean choosing a tiny market forever. It means choosing a sharper starting point so you can get traction faster, learn faster, and build something that feels obvious to the right people.

Most products expand later. Very few products start broad successfully.

The easiest way to niche down is to narrow by one meaningful variable at a time.

| Niching angle | What it means | Example niche | Why it works early |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Company size | Narrow by revenue, team size, or complexity | Shopify brands doing $100k+/month | Similar budgets, urgency, and needs |
| Vertical | Narrow by industry or category | Beauty brands on Shopify | Shared language, workflows, and pain points |
| Stage | Narrow by where they are in the journey | Pre-launch founders | Clear immediate priorities |
| Geography | Narrow by country or region | EU ecommerce brands | Compliance, taxes, and local needs |
| Business model | Narrow by how they make money | Subscription brands | Different incentives and KPIs |
| Platform | Narrow by tools they already use | Notion creators | Easier distribution and integrations |
| Job role | Narrow by who owns the problem | Ecommerce retention managers | Clear buyer and use case |
| Use case | Narrow by one urgent outcome | Recover abandoned carts | Instantly understandable value |
| Pain intensity | Narrow by who feels it most | Agencies wasting hours on reports | Pain drives faster action |

Most founders only niche down once. Stronger positioning usually comes from combining two angles.

**Examples:**

- Shopify beauty brands
- EU subscription brands
- Agencies managing 10+ stores
- Pre-launch founders building AI apps

Shopify merchants (Too broad) → Shopify beauty brands (Better) → Shopify beauty brands doing $100k+/month needing subscription retention (Sharp)

<Callout>
**Bonus:** When you niche down marketing *should* get cheaper and easier. Ads convert better. Content converts better. Outreach gets easier. Referrals improve because people know exactly who to send you to.
</Callout>

You are not locking yourself into this niche forever. You are choosing the fastest path to traction. Once you win somewhere specific, expansion gets much easier.

## Look for buying behavior, not labels

Job titles and demographics can be useful, but they rarely tell the full story. Behavior is more revealing.

Who is already spending money to solve this problem? Who is patching together tools every week? Who has tried other solutions and is still frustrated?

Those people are often better customers than someone who simply "fits the demographic."

Pain creates action. Action creates opportunity.

---

## If you cannot picture them, you do not know them

> "Freelancers" is vague.

> "Freelance designers juggling five clients and tracking deliverables in spreadsheets" is useful.

The second version tells you more about workflows, pain points, pricing sensitivity, and what the product should do. You should be able to picture a real person using your product in a real moment.

If you cannot, your audience definition is still too soft.

---

## Can you find three of them today?

A strong audience is findable.

You should be able to name at least three real people who match your target user right now. If you cannot think of anyone, search becomes harder, outreach becomes harder, and validation becomes slower.

Good places to look:

- LinkedIn
- niche communities
- Reddit
- Facebook groups
- Slack or Discord groups
- your existing network

If real people are hard to find, the audience may still be too vague or you just don't understand them enough which will make it really hard to sell to them.

---

## Talk before you build

Many founders spend months refining ideas when a few conversations would reveal more in a week.

You do not need a pitch. You need honesty.

A simple message works:

> Hey [name], I'm researching how people handle [problem]. Curious how you're doing it today and where it still feels painful.

You are listening for patterns:

- repeated frustrations
- messy workarounds
- budget already being spent
- urgency to improve it now

That is better signal than compliments.

---

## Mistakes founders make here

The most common mistake is choosing an audience based on access instead of pain. It is easier to build for people you know. That does not mean they need the product most.

Another mistake is chasing broad markets too early because they seem bigger.

Big markets are useful later. Early on, clarity usually beats size.

And be careful with random interest. A few people outside your target group may sign up or ask questions. That does not mean they should shape the roadmap.

---

## A quick scorecard

Your audience is probably strong if:

- they feel the problem often
- they can describe the frustration clearly
- they are already trying to solve it
- you know where to reach more of them
- they would notice value quickly

If several of those are missing, narrow further.

---

## The takeaway

Your ideal customer is not the largest group available. It is the group most likely to care first. Find the people with sharp pain, clear urgency, and reachable channels. Build for them well.

Expansion is easier after traction.
