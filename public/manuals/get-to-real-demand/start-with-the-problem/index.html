Section 01

Start with the problem

You do not need a genius idea. You need a problem that is specific, painful, and already causing friction.

AI has made building easier than ever. Anyone can create working products, prototypes, and internal tools quickly. That means weak ideas get exposed faster.

If the problem is minor, people will build a workaround, patch together tools, or ignore it. The hard part is no longer building. The hard part is choosing a problem worth building for.

That is what this section helps you do.

Why this matters

A vague problem creates vague everything. Messaging feels generic. Product decisions become guesswork. Validation gets noisy. Scope expands for no reason.

Compare these two ideas:

"Small businesses need better marketing tools."

"Shopify brands with repeat customers lose revenue because no post-purchase winback emails are set up."

The second version is clearer, more useful, and easier to build around.

When people are already trying to solve a problem, you are not inventing demand. You are meeting it.

AI changed the bar

AI did not just make building easier. It changed how people evaluate software.

Before, users often tolerated clunky UX, confusing onboarding, or expensive pricing because alternatives were limited. Now they compare your product to building something themselves.

That raises the bar for every new product.

A problem that felt strong enough before may not be strong enough now. If the pain is minor, infrequent, or easy to workaround, many users will not pay for software.

Strong opportunities usually involve real stakes: lost revenue, wasted time, recurring friction, trust risk, or complexity that is hard to solve alone.

You are no longer just asking:

Is this a real problem?

You are also asking:

Is this a real problem someone would rather pay to solve than solve themselves?

💡

Write one sentence explaining why someone would choose a paid solution instead of solving this problem themselves.

What's actually broken

Describe the problem in plain language. No features. No solution. Just what is not working.

Weak framing:

"Reporting is annoying."

Better framing:

"Founders check three tools every week just to understand churn."

Another example:

"Free users sign up but never complete the setup flow."

A good problem is observable and repeatable. If you cannot point to where it happens or how it hurts someone, it is probably too vague.

💡

Write one sentence describing what's broken, including when it happens and what goes wrong.

Who feels it most

Not everyone feels the same problem equally. Some notice it occasionally. Others deal with it every day.

You are looking for the group where the problem is frequent, frustrating, and tied to real consequences like lost time or missed revenue.

"Sending emails" is broad.

"Shopify brands doing $50k/month with no retention flows" is useful.

"SaaS founders with free users but no activation emails" is useful.

The more specific the group, the easier product decisions, messaging, and growth become.

💡

Write down who feels this problem most and why now. It can be a best guess for now.

How they solve it today

Every real problem already has a solution, even if it is a bad one.

Usually it looks like spreadsheets, manual work, multiple tools stitched together, or repetitive tasks handled by humans.

Examples:

  • exporting CSVs weekly to build reports
  • manually sending winback emails
  • checking multiple tools just to answer one question
  • copying leads from forms into a CRM

That is not edge-case behavior. That is demand hiding in plain sight.

If someone says, "we use three tools and a spreadsheet to make this work," pay attention.

💡

List the current ways people are solving this today, even if they are messy or inefficient.

Why it's not good enough

This is where opportunity shows up.

You are looking for a problem that still feels unresolved.

Maybe the tool works, but setup takes two hours. Maybe reporting exists, but nobody trusts the data. Maybe the product solves the issue, but the UX is confusing and overpriced.

If people are still frustrated after trying to solve it, there is room to build something better.

💡

Identify the top 2–3 frustrations in the current solution.

What you're looking for

Strong problems tend to share a few traits:

  • they happen often
  • they cost time or money
  • people are already solving them badly
  • the current solution feels incomplete
  • users would rather pay than keep patching it together

If most of those are true, you are in a good place to move forward.

Case study: sharpen the pain, not the product

A weak version of an idea might be:

Build a habit tracker where people upload progress photos.

Useful, but forgettable.

A stronger version might be:

Build a private glow-up tracker for women documenting 30/60/90 day transformation journeys during moments like wedding prep, postpartum resets, or confidence rebuilds.

The core mechanic barely changed. Users still upload progress photos.

What changed was the problem.

Now there is a clear audience, emotional motivation, recurring use, and stronger positioning.

Many founders keep changing the product when they should be sharpening the pain.

The takeaway

Start with what's broken.

Everything else — the customer, the product, the positioning, and the go-to-market path — gets easier once that is clear.

In an AI world, weak problems get exposed faster. Strong problems become clearer faster too.

Frequently asked questions
When should I quit my job to work on my startup?

Quit when the evidence forces your hand — paying customers, real demand, and enough runway to focus. Not when the idea feels exciting.

How do I know if my problem is "real enough"?

You can name three people experiencing it this week, describe how they're solving it today, and explain what it costs them to keep doing that.

Should I validate before I build?

Yes. Spend a week talking to people in the problem before you spend a month building a solution. The fastest builders are the ones who validate fastest.
